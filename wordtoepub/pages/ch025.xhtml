Key Players in the Cloud

The adoption and evolution of cloud computing involve multiple stakeholders, similar to those found in traditional ITC service delivery. Since cloud computing relies on the same fundamental technologies, the main difference lies in the shift of responsibility towards software, as the hardware layer is abstracted by the cloud.

This chapter will focus on the supply and procurement processes of public cloud resources for enterprise services.

Consumer-oriented cloud services (such as Gmail or Office 365) represent a simplified version of cloud service sales, with similar but less stringent regulations from the customer's perspective. For instance, an individual customer is not required to comply with GDPR regulations when managing their personal data in the cloud.

To analyze these roles, we introduce a powerful yet simplified tool: the RACI matrix, used for mapping the chain of responsibility in managing a cloud-native information ecosystem.

The Responsibility Matrix

Identifying the key players in cloud service management is essential to defining the responsibility chain for service delivery.

This responsibility chain is typically represented as a two-dimensional matrix that describes who is accountable for what within a given context.

  • Columns (usually fewer in number) represent the stakeholders involved in the analysis.

  • Rows list critical activities.

  • Cells define RACI roles (in a simplified or multidimensional format), mapping the lifecycle management of the cloud ecosystem and its resources.

The RACI Classification

In the simplified version, we use the RACI model, where each letter stands for a specific role:

  • Responsible (R) – The individual or team responsible for completing the task. Multiple responsible parties may be assigned to an activity.

  • Accountable (A) – The final authority who ensures the task is correctly completed and approved . Each activity must have only one Accountable .

  • Consulted (C) – Experts whose opinions are required for decision-making. Communication with them is bidirectional .

  • Informed (I) – Individuals who need to stay updated on progress but are not directly involved . Communication with them is unidirectional .

Can One Person Hold Multiple Roles?

Yes, this is possible in agile, cross-functional organizations, where responsibilities are distributed at the team level.

However, it is crucial to distinguish between the person responsible for executing a task (R) and the one approving it (A).

The Consulted (C) and Informed (I) roles should also be clearly identified to ensure proper governance.

Dependencies Between Services

In a cloud information ecosystem, a service like A-1 may depend on third-party services, while simultaneously serving as a dependency for others.

This is illustrated in Figure 28 – Dependencies Between Services and Processes.

A RACI matrix can map these dependencies, assigning Accountable (A) roles to ensure that the release of Service A-1-1 does not disrupt Service A-1.

Similarly, A-1 could be included in a RACI matrix for Service A, ensuring that higher-level dependencies are accounted for.

For industrial process architectures, integrating these matrices digitally can enhance automated non-regression testing and compliance validation.

  • Dependency identification must be established during design and continuously maintained.

  • Automated tools can help map dependencies and trigger corrective actions.

  • Artificial Intelligence cannot yet make intuitive decisions, but it can process extensive datasets to support decision-making.

  • Final decisions should always involve a chief process reviewer within the company.

Cloud Supply Chain

To understand procurement, we must first analyze the cloud supply chain, which follows a structured hierarchy...

Figure 29 - Cloud Supply Chain

Primary Cloud Provider

The primary cloud provider is the legal entity that owns and delivers cloud services.

  • It is responsible for all cloud services provided.

  • It ensures compliance with national and international regulations as per its sales contracts.

  • It subcontracts core infrastructure services within its global data center network.

However, the primary cloud provider is NOT responsible for:

  • Data traffic between the customer and the provider’s data centers.

  • Application-level service availability if the customer builds a custom application.

  • Application-layer security for services developed by the customer.

For PaaS and SaaS models, the cloud provider ensures:

  • Confidentiality and data security within its cloud resources.

  • Compliance with privacy and governance frameworks.

For IaaS or hybrid services, responsibilities are more limited, always defined per resource type and documented.

Third-Party Cloud Services & Marketplaces

Third-party services hosted on cloud marketplaces introduce a more complex responsibility matrix.

  • Many marketplace applications run on cloud resources acquired by the consumer.

  • Some services auto-deploy resources within the customer's environment, adding complexity.

.

Cloud Service Distribution Model

Cloud providers often outsource distribution to regional market players, including:

  • Distributors – Provide technical and commercial support to local markets.

  • Resellers – Act as system integrators, ensuring smooth customer adoption.

Enterprise Cloud Adoption Challenges

Large enterprises often separate procurement centers from technical centers of excellence (COE), creating inefficiencies in cloud adoption.

To address this, cloud experts play a Consulted (C) role in the RACI matrix for cloud architecture decisions.

In summary, an efficient cloud supply chain requires clear accountability, robust procurement strategies, and technical governance, ensuring the business units receive cloud resources efficiently.
